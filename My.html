My First Inspiration

Why do the scented bowers
In fragrant fray
Rival each other's flowers
This festive day?

Rizal first begins by showing an image of a garden around him. He describes the “scented bowers” or garden alcoves, which are competing with each other in terms of fragrance and beauty. This competition among the flowers is depicted as a celebration of a special event.

Why is sweet melody bruited
In the sylvan dale,
Harmony sweet and fluted
- Like the nightingale?

Rizal then notices the presence of sweet melodies in the “sylvan dale” or a wooded valley. He describes the harmony as being as sweet as the song of a nightingale, suggesting a joyful and peaceful atmosphere.

Why do the birds sing so
In the tender grass,
Flitting from bough to bough
With the winds that pass?

He continues to observe the birds singing joyfully and flitting from branch to branch. The “tender grass” and the gentle passing of the winds add to the scene’s serenity and liveliness, suggesting a celebratory mood in nature.

And why does the crystal spring
Run among the flowers
While lullaby zephyrs sing
Like its crystal showers?

The poet reflects on the beauty of the crystal-clear spring flowing through the flowers. The “lullaby zephyrs” or gentle breezes sing softly, creating a soothing atmosphere.

I see the dawn in the East
With beauty endowed.
Why goes she to a feast
In a carmine cloud?

Here, Rizal continues the serene theme of the poem, personifying the dawn breaking in the East as a beautiful figure going to a feast adorned in a “carmine cloud”, which could represent the colors of the sunrise.

Sweet mother, they celebrate
Your natal day

Here, it is revealed that the celebration he describes is in honor of his mother’s birthday.

The rose with her scent innate,
The bird with his lay.

He describes how the flowers and the birds participate in this celebration with their scents and songs.

The murmurous spring this day
Without alloy,
Murmuring bids you always
To live in joy.

Rizal speaks of a “murmurous spring” which could be a metaphor for a gentle flow of water. This spring seems to murmur a message to his mother, wishing her a life filled with joy.

While the crystalline murmurs glisten,
Hear you the accents strong
Struck from my lyre, listen!
To my love's first song.

In the final stanza, Rizal urges his mother to listen to the murmurs of the spring, which symbolize his own wishes for his mother. He refers to his poem as “my love’s first song”, indicating that this poem is an expression of his love and admiration for his mother.
