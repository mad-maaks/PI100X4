To the Child Jesus

How, God-Child hast Thou come
To earth in cave forlorn?

The term “God-Child” refers to Jesus, emphasizing both his divinity as God, and his humanity as a child. Rizal expresses his wonder about Jesus’ birth. The imagery of a “cave forlorn” emphasizes the difference between Jesus’ divine nature, and the humble conditions of how he was born into this world.

Does Fortune now deride Thee
When Thou art scarcely born?

These lines express Rizal’s astonishment that Jesus, despite being divine, is born into humble and lowly conditions. It’s a contemplative remark on the irony and mystery of the divine choosing to be born into poverty and humility.

Ah, woe! Celestial King,
- Who mortal from dost keep
.Woulds't rather than be Sovereign
Be Shepherd of Thy Sheep?

Here, we see Rizal expressing a sense of sorrow or pity for Jesus, whom he calls the “Celestial King.” That despite being a ruler who could remain separate from human suffering, Jesus chooses to be a humble shepherd. It reflects Rizal’s view of Jesus who chooses to serve humanity rather than sovereignty.
