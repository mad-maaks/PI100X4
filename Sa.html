Sa Aking Mga Kababata

Kapagka ang baya'y sadyang umiibig
sa kanyang salitang kaloob ng langit,
sanlang kalayaan nasa ring masapit
katulad ng ibong nasa himpapawid.

Pagkat ang salita'y isang kahatulan
sa bayan, sa nayo't mga kaharian,
at ang isang tao'y katulad, kabagay
ng alin mang likha noong kalayaan.

Ang hindi magmahal sa kanyang salita
mahigit sa hayop at malansang isda,
kaya ang marapat pagyamaning kusa
na tulad sa isang tunay na nagpala.

Ang wikang tagalog tulad din sa latin,
sa ingles, kastila at salitang anghel
sapagka't ang Poong maalam tumingin
ang siyang naggawad, nagbigay sa atin.

Ang salita nati'y huwad din sa iba
na may alfabeto at sariling letra,
na kaya nawala'y dinatnan ng sigwa
ang lunday sa lawa noong dakong una.

Did You Know?

This poem was written by Jose Rizal at the age of eight years old, while he was a student at Ateneo Municipal de Manila.

Sa Aking Mga Kababata

As one of his early works, it is significant because it offers insight into Rizal’s thoughts and feelings about his country at a very young age.

Sa Aking Mga Kababata

This poem reflects his deep sense of patriotism and concern for the future of the Philippines.

Sa Aking Mga Kababata

To My Fellow Children

Whenever people of a country truly love
The language which by heav'n they were taught to use
That country also surely liberty pursue
As does the bird which soars to freer space above.

The poem begins by highlighting the significance of loving one’s own language. He refers to the native language as a gift from heaven or of divine and natural origin, making it a vital part of a nation’s identity.

For language is the final judge and referee
Upon the people in the land where it holds sway;

Here, Rizal portrays language as a crucial factor in defining and judging a person’s identity. He emphasizes the power and influence of a language in shaping the culture and identity of a nation.

In truth our human race resembles in this way
The other living beings born in liberty.

He notes that there is a parallel between humans and other creatures, emphasizing that language is an intrinsic part of freedom and natural existence.

Whoever knows not how to love his native tongue
Is worse than any best or evil smelling fish.

Here, we see one of the most famous lines of Rizal. These lines use strong imagery to convey disdain for those who reject their native language, comparing them to a lowly creature.

To make our language richer ought to be our wish
The same as any mother loves to feed her young.

Rizal then advocates for the development and enrichment of the native language. He compares this nurturing of language to a mother’s care for her children, suggesting that it is a natural and essential duty.

Tagalog and the Latin language are the same
And English and Castilian and the angels' tongue;

He further equates Tagalog with English and Spanish, as well as the “angels’ tongue;” he is essentially emphasizing linguistic equality.

And God, whose watchful care o'er all is flung,
Has given us His blessing in the speech we claim,

Here, Rizal reinforces the idea that the native language is a divine gift, deserving of respect and appreciation.

Our mother tongue, like all the highest that we know
Had alphabet and letters of its very own;
But these were lost -- by furious waves were overthrown
Like bancas in the stormy sea, long years ago.

Rizal highlights the richness of the native language, and laments the loss of these due to “furious waves”, which in the context of when this poem was written, could mean the Spanish oppressors. Here, we also see the metaphor of “bancas” or small boats being overturned in a stormy sea, which could symbolize the cultural and linguistic upheaval that the Philippines experienced at that time.
