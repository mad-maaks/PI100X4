Mi Retiro

Cabe anchurosa playa de fina y suave arena
y al pie de una montaña cubierta de verdor
planté mi choza humilde bajo arboleda amena,
buscando de los bosques en la quietud serena
reposo a mi cerebro, silencio a mi dolor.

Su techo es frágil su suelo débil cana,
sus vigas y columnas maderas sin labrar;
nada vale, por cierto, mi rústica cabaña;
mas duerme en el regazo de la eterna montaña,
y la canta y la arrulla noche y día el mar.

Un afluente arroyuelo, que de la selva umbria
desciende entre peñascos, la baña con amor,
y un chorro le regala por tosca cañería
que en la cálida noche es canto y melodía
y néctar cristalino del día en el calor.

Si el cielo esta sereno, mansa corre la fuente,
Su cítara invisible tañendo sin cesar;
pero vienen las lluvias, e impetuoso torrente
peñas y abismos salta, ronco, espumante, hirviente,
y se arroja rugiendo frenético hacia el mar.

Del perro los ladridos, de las aves trino
del kalao la voz ronca solas se oyen alli,
no hay hombre vanidoso ni importuno vecino
que se imponga a mi mente, ni estorbo mi camino;
solo tengo las selvas y el mar cerca de mí.

¡El mar, el mar es todo! su masa soberana
los átomos me trae de mundos que lejos son;
me alienta su sonrisa de límpida mañana,
y cuando por la tarde mi fe resulta vana
encuentra en sus tristezas un eco el corazón.

Did You Know?

It is a poem that Jose Rizal wrote during his exile in Dapitan, a town where he was banished by the Spanish government from 1892 to 1896.

Mi Retiro

This poem is a reflection of his life in exile, the nature surrounding him, and his philosophical musings.

Mi Retiro

My Retreat

By the spreading beach where the sands are soft and fine,
At the foot of the mount in its mantle of green
I have built my hut in the pleasant grove's confine;

Rizal opens his poem with a vivid description of his exile location in Dapitan. Here, we see a beautiful description of the beach, the mountains, and a peaceful grove where he had built his simple hut.

From the forest seeking peace and a calmness divine,
Rest for the weary brain and silence to my sorrow keen.

The repeated emphasis on “peace” and “calmness” shows Rizal’s desire to escape the turmoil of his exile. Nature acts as a resting place for his “weary brain” and his “sorrow keen”.

Its roof of the frail palm leaf and its floor the cane.
Its beams and posts of the unhewn wood;
Little there is of value in this hut so plain,
And better by far in the lap of the mount to have lain,
By the song and the murmur of the high sea's flood.

Here, Rizal describes his hut as a basic structure made of materials, such as, palm leaves, cane, and unhewn wood. He uses this to suggest that the true value lies in the beauty of the environment, not in material possessions, as it provides him more comfort and peace.

A purling brook from the woodland glade
Drops down o'er the stones and around it sweeps,
Whence a fresh stream is a drawn by the rough cane's aid;
That in the still night its' murmur has made,
And in the day's heat a crystal fountain leaps.

He then describes a brook flowing through the woods, providing him a source of fresh water. Rizal personifies this stream, describing its gentle murmur at night, and lively flow during the day, reflecting the serenity of nature.

When the sky is serene how gently it flows,
And its zither unseen ceaselessly plays;
But when the rains fall a torrent it goes
Boiling and foaming through the rocky close,
Roaring uncheck'd to the sea's wide ways.

Rizal continues the serene description of the environment he was in, however, he also adds the contrasting nature of the brook during strong storms. Here, he creates a vivid image of the brook’s power during rain, emphasizing on the roar of the water, which could symbolize untamed emotions. These passages reflect the peacefulness and wildness that nature offers.

The howl of the dog and the song of the bird,
And only the kalao's hoarse call resound;
Nor is the voice of vain man to be heard;

Here, he focuses on the sounds of nature, of birds, dogs, and the “kalao” which breaks the silence around him. This solitude separates him from the “vain man”and his troubles. It allows him for self-reflection and a sense of freedom.

My mind to harass or my steps to begird;
The woodlands alone and the sea wrap me round.

These lines suggest that Rizal is completely isolated from the distractions and demands of society. The use of “harass” implies a constant mental pressure, while “begird” means to encircle, suggesting a physical limitation. In this context, Rizal is stating that neither his thoughts nor movements are restricted. These lines also further suggest how nature is providing him solace and refuge.

The sea, ah, the sea! for me it is all,
And it massively sweeps from the world's apart;

The poem shifts its focus to the sea this time, which becomes a symbol of his separation from the outside world because of his exile.

Its smile in the morn to my soul is a call,
And when in the evening my faith seems to pall,
It breathes with its sadness an echo to my heart.

He describes the sea here with changing moods, a smile in the morning and sadness at night, mirroring Rizal’s own emotional state. This poem showcases both the harshness and serenity of his surrounding environment, a reflection of Rizal’s reality during his exile.
