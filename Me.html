Me Piden Versos

Piden que pulse la lira
Ha tiempo callada y rota:
Si ya no arranco una nota
Ni mi musa ya me inspira!
Balbuce fría y delira
Si la tortura mi mente;
Cuando ríe solo miente;
Como miente su lamento:
Y es que en mi triste aislamiento
Mi alma ni goza ni siente.

Hubo un tiempo ... y es verdad!
Pero ya aquel tiempo huyó,
En que vate me llamo
La indulgencia a la amistad.
Ahora de aquella edad
El recuerdo apenas resta
Como quedan de una fiesta
Los misteriosos sonidos
Que retienen los oídos
Del bullicio de la orquesta.

Soy planta apenas crecida
Arrancada del Oriente,
Donde es perfume el ambiente,
Donde es un sueño la vida:
Patria que jamás se olvida!
Enseñáronme a cantar
Las aves, con su trinar;
Con su rumor, las cascadas;
Y en sus playas dilatadas,
Los murmullos de la mar.

They Asked Me for Verses

They ask me to play on a lyre
That long has been still and decayed,
But never a note have I played,
Nor can I the Muse re-inspire.

The poem starts with the speaker being asked to write poetry. The “lyre” here is often a symbol of poetry and music, representing Rizal’s past creative expression. The fact that it is “still and decayed” suggests that Rizal hasn’t written poetry in a while. He expresses his struggles in writing, and how he can’t get his inspiration and motivation to write back.

She chats without reason or fire
Until she has tortured my brain.

These lines suggest that Rizal’s poetry lacks the “reason or fire” needed for good poetry. This lack of inspiration becomes a torment for Rizal, his muse, instead of helping, “tortures” him with the struggle to create.

She chuckles to jeer at my pain;
She has mocked me the while I lamented.

Rizal then personifies the muse as mocking, she doesn’t seem to care about Rizal’s suffering and even laughs at it. The muse offers no comfort to him, only mockery.

In my soul, lonely, sad, and tormented,
Neither pleasure nor sorrow remain.

Here, we can see how the effect of the lack of inspiration for Rizal takes its toll on him. He expresses that his soul feels “lonely, sad, and tormented”. The struggle to create has drained him so much, that he feels devoid of joy or pain.

There once was a time, it is true--
A time that, alas, has departed.
When friends who were generous-hearted,
Applauded the verse I could do.

The poem then shifts to a reminiscing tone, he describes the time when his works were appreciated by his friends, how he was admired for his poetry. He describes this in a very melancholic way, stating that this memory has long since departed.

Of those happy days but a few
Obscured recollections yet stay,
As after some high holiday,
Still linger mysterious sounds;

Rizal then says how those good times are fading to him, that only a few remain. Despite this, those memories still linger to him like faint sounds after a celebration, although unclear.

Or, after the concert resounds,
The after tones whisper away.

He further emphasizes the fading nature of his past, here. He describes this with the “concert resounds”, how the “after tones” will gradually fade away.

For I am a plant immature,
Torn out of the Orient where

Rizal then compares himself to a young plant, uprooted from its natural environment. This represents how he must have felt during the time he was writing this, when he was away from the Philippines.

The perfumes sleep on the air
And life is a dream to allure.
Ah, memories ever endure,

These lines evoke a sense of peace and beauty in his homeland; this is symbolized by the “perfumes” sleeping in the air. He further describes the Philippines as if it was a dream, suggesting how he misses his homeland and how it is his source of inspiration.

My Country, of songs taught to me
By warbling birds from the tree,
The waterfall's silvery roar,

Here, Rizal directly speaks about his homeland, the Philippines, how he cherishes his memories of his time there, and how this is what fuels his creative spirit.

And out on the far-reaching shore,
The moan of the sounding sea.

The poem ends with the image of the ocean’s sound, another reminder of his homeland. In this poem, he explores his struggle with writer’s block, and the power of memory and his homeland in fueling his creative passion.
