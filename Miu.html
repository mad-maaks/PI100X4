Mi Ultimo Adios

Adios, Patria adorada, region del sol querida,
Perla del Mar de Oriente, nuestro perdido Eden!
A darte voy alegre la triste mustia vida,
Y fuera más brillante más fresca, más florida,
Tambien por tí la diera, la diera por tu bien.

En campos de batalla, luchando con delirio
Otros te dan sus vidas sin dudas, sin pesar;
El sitio nada importa, ciprés, laurel ó lirio,
Cadalso ó campo abierto, combate ó cruel martirio,
Lo mismo es si lo piden la patria y el hogar.

Yo muero cuando veo que el cielo se colora
Y al fin anuncia el día trás lóbrego capuz;
Si grana necesitas para teñir tu aurora,
Vierte la sangre mía, derrámala en buen hora
Y dórela un reflejo de su naciente luz.

Mis sueños cuando apenas muchacho adolescente,
Mis sueños cuando joven ya lleno de vigor,
Fueron el verte un día, joya del mar de oriente
Secos los negros ojos, alta la tersa frente,
Sin ceño, sin arrugas, sin manchas de rubor.

Ensueño de mi vida, mi ardiente vivo anhelo,
Salud te grita el alma que pronto va á partir!
Salud! ah que es hermoso caer por darte vuelo,
Morir por darte vida, morir bajo tu cielo,
Y en tu encantada tierra la eternidad dormir.

Si sobre mi sepulcro vieres brotar un dia
Entre la espesa yerba sencilla, humilde flor,
Acércala a tus labios y besa al alma mía,
Y sienta yo en mi frente bajo la tumba fría
De tu ternura el soplo, de tu hálito el calor.

Deja a la luna verme con luz tranquila y suave;
Deja que el alba envíe su resplandor fugaz,
Deja gemir al viento con su murmullo grave,
Y si desciende y posa sobre mi cruz un ave,
Deja que el ave entone su cantico de paz.

Did You Know?

This poem was written by Jose Rizal shortly before his execution on December 30, 1896.

Mi Ultimo Adios

It has since become one of his most famous works, and is considered a national treasure in the Philippines.

Mi Ultimo Adios

The poem is a heartfelt farewell to his beloved country and a reflection of his life, beliefs, and hopes for the future of the Philippines.

Mi Ultimo Adios

My Last Farewell

Farewell, my adored Land, region of the sun caressed,
Pearl of the Orient Sea, our Eden lost,

The poem opens by directly addressing his homeland, the Philippines. He compares the Philippines to a precious pearl and a lost paradise, emphasizing the value and beauty of the Philippines, along with a longing for its potential.

With gladness I give you my Life, sad and repressed;
And were it more brilliant, more fresh and at its best,
I would still give it to you for your welfare at most.

Here, Rizal expresses his willingness to sacrifice his life for his country, despite the sadness and repressions he faced, highlighting his selfless love and unwavering commitment to the Philippines.

On the fields of battle, in the fury of fight,
Others give you their lives without pain or hesitancy,

He further emphasizes how dying for one’s country is an honorable act, similar to those who die in war. Then, he acknowledges the people who have already died for the country, and expresses his respect towards them.

The place does not matter: cypress laurel, lily white,
Scaffold, open field, conflict or martyrdom's site,
It is the same if asked by home and Country.

Laurels, often depicted as a symbol of victory, glory, and honor, and white lilies, symbolizing purity, are both used here as symbolic representations of either a heroic or peaceful demise. However, he emphasizes that the death itself does not matter in comparison to the cause. He reinforces the idea that the measure of the sacrifice lies solely in its dedication to the nation.

I die as I see tints on the sky b'gin to show
And at last announce the day, after a gloomy night;

The first line suggests that even though he is dying, there is still hope for the Philippines’ liberation. The “gloomy night” from the second line represents the Philippines’ current state, while the new day signifies the possibility of freedom. Here, we see Rizal express his belief for a brighter future for the Philippines, despite his nearing death.

If you need a hue to dye your matutinal glow,
Pour my blood and at the right moment spread it so,
And gild it with a reflection of your nascent light!

Rizal uses a metaphor of offering his own blood to color the dawn in order to express his desire to be a part of the Philippines’ liberation. He concludes the third stanza by expressing his hope that his death will contribute to the Philippines’ bright future.

My dreams, when scarcely a lad adolescent,
My dreams when already a youth, full of vigor to attain,
Were to see you, gem of the sea of the Orient,
Your dark eyes dry, smooth brow held to a high plane
Without frown, without wrinkles and of shame without stain.

Here, Rizal talks about his childhood dreams, and his aspirations for the country he loves. He talks about his lifelong dream to see a free and prosperous Philippines, and desires it to be rid of suffering and humiliation.

My life's fancy, my ardent, passionate desire,
Hail! Cries out the soul to you, that will soon part from thee;
Hail! How sweet 'tis to fall that fullness you may acquire;

He further expresses his deepest wishes for the Philippines, and bids farewell to the land he loves as his time is nearing. Rizal states here how dying for his country is a sweet sacrifice to him.

To die to give you life, 'neath your skies to expire,
And in your mystic land to sleep through eternity!

Rizal further expresses the love he has for the country, ready to give his life for its betterment. He also shows his desire here, for his death and legacy to forever be connected to his beloved Philippines.

If over my tomb some day, you would see blow,
A simple humble flow'r amidst thick grasses,
Bring it up to your lips and kiss my soul so,

Here, Rizal creates an image of a flower growing on his grave, symbolizing simplicity and humility in his sacrifice. He asks his countrymen to honor him by kissing the flower, a gesture of respect and remembrance of him.

And under the cold tomb, I may feel on my brow,
Warmth of your breath, a whiff of your tenderness.

He states that he may feel the warmth of the people’s love and respect towards him, and expresses his desire to be remembered with tenderness and warmth. This reflects the deep bond between him and his country.

Let the moon with soft, gentle light me descry,
Let the dawn send forth its fleeting, brilliant light,

He envisions the moon’s light shining on his grave, which symbolizes peace and serenity. He also imagines the dawn bringing new hope and life, a metaphor for the Philippines’ future.

In murmurs grave allow the wind to sigh,
And should a bird descend on my cross and alight,
Let the bird intone a song of peace o'er my site.

Finally, he shows an image of the wind whispering solemnly, a bird resting on his grave, and a peaceful song sung over his resting place, all representing the peace and freedom that he hoped for his country.
